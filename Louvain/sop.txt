


1. The Louvain algorithm is sensitive to node ordering, leading to unstable and non-deterministic community detection results.
-The Louvain algorithm is an inherently stochastic method, meaning it can produce vastly different community partitions each time it is run on the exact same network (Pankratz et al., 2024). This non-deterministic behavior occurs because community detection algorithms often rely on randomized heuristic procedures to navigate massive solution spaces (Morea & De Stefano, 2024). Specifically, the Louvain algorithm is highly sensitive to initial conditions, such as the starting partition of the network, and its final output is strictly dependent on the random sequence in which individual nodes are examined and processed (Wang et al., 2024). Because the algorithm identifies a local modularity maximum based on this randomized node ordering, multiple executions will yield highly variable and unstable community structures (Morea & De Stefano, 2024; Wang et al., 2024). Ultimately, this reliance on random initialization and local decision-making not only compromises the consistency of the results but can also lead to the creation of weakly connected or internally disconnected communities (Pankratz et al., 2024).

2. The Louvain algorithm relies on modularity optimization, which introduces a resolution limit that prevents the detection of smaller communities within large-scale networks.
-Although the Louvain method is a highly efficient algorithm for community detection, its fundamental reliance on modularity maximization makes it susceptible to the well-known resolution limit problem (Sahu, 2023; Yao et al., 2023). Because of this inherent flaw, optimizing for Q-modularity fails to identify or resolve communities that fall below a specific scale or size threshold (Gharaghooshi et al., 2020; Sahu, 2023; Yao et al., 2023). Consequently, the algorithm often obscures fine-grained structures by incorrectly merging nodes from distinctly different small communities into larger groups in its final partition (Yao et al., 2023). Furthermore, this resolution limit stands in direct contrast to the field of view limit, a separate structural issue that causes the overpartitioning of communities with large diameters (Gharaghooshi et al., 2020).

. Using a "ring of cliques" benchmark network, the simulation illustrates how the Louvain algorithm is biased toward grouping smaller, distinct sub-communities into larger partitions
. Because modularity evaluates internal links relative to the total number of links in the entire global network, the algorithm incorrectly merges structurally meaningful cliques simply because they fall below the resolution limit
.


3. The Louvain algorithm employs a greedy, local optimization strategy that fails to guarantee globally optimal community partitions.
-The Louvain algorithm operates on a greedy optimization process that iteratively updates community assignments node by node to maximize immediate, local modularity gains (Wang & Kolter, 2020). Because these reassignments are evaluated solely on localized gains without enforcing internal connectivity constraints, the algorithm violates fundamental structural assumptions and can produce internally non-cohesive or completely disconnected communities (Santos et al., 2025; Wang & Kolter, 2020). This reliance on local decision-making makes the algorithm highly susceptible to getting trapped in local optima, a limitation that is particularly evident when analyzing complex networks with nuanced structures (Hernández et al., 2025; Wang & Kolter, 2020).
Furthermore, because the algorithm applies these greedy updates sequentially during its local moving phase, the final partition is highly sensitive to the exact order in which nodes are processed. Consequently, different node orderings can yield entirely different partitions that share comparable modularity scores, introducing a level of non-determinism that compromises the reproducibility of the results (Santos et al., 2025).

Panel A shows a connected community containing a central node that acts as a bridge between two sub-groups. Panel B shows the network after this bridge node is reassigned to an adjacent community, leaving the original community split into two entirely disconnected groups of nodes.]
Figure 1.3 shows that the Louvain algorithm's strict reliance on maximizing immediate, local modularity gains can produce arbitrarily poorly connected or entirely disconnected communities
. In the first stage, a specific node acts as a bridge connecting different components of its assigned community
. In the next stage, the algorithm greedily reassigns this bridge node to a neighboring community because the move offers a higher immediate modularity score
. Because the algorithm does not evaluate the long-term global impact of this move or enforce internal connectivity constraints, the remaining nodes stay in their current assignment, leaving their community structurally fragmented and internally disconnected
.
